Motivation and Objective

As a major methodology for handling parametric uncertainties in dynamical systems, adaptive control/estimation has attracted much attention in both academia and industry over the past few decades. The classical adaptive control/estimation usually achieves only asymptotic error convergence in the absence of a condition termed persistent excitation (PE). The PE condition requires the persistent richness of system input-output signals, which is too stringent to be satisfied in practice. Even when PE exists, the rate of parameter convergence is highly dependent on the PE strength, generally resulting in a slow learning process. For these reasons, classical adaptive control/estimation usually exhibits poor transient performance and is fragile in complicated environments. Another fundamental limitation of the classical adaptive control/estimation is that it imposes appropriate structural knowledge on parametric uncertainties, which is generally assumed to come into the system dynamics in a linear or monotonic manner, such that the standard gradient descent search is applicable to the online parameter identification loop. However, this hinders the effective handling of complicated systems. The above limitations prevent most existing adaptive algorithms from widespread applications in real-world systems.
